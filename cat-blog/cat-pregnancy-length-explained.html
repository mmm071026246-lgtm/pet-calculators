Cat Pregnancy Length Explained

Cat pregnancy typically lasts between 63 and 67 days, although some pregnancies may be slightly shorter or longer. Understanding the pregnancy timeline can help owners prepare for the arrival of kittens and provide proper care for the mother.

How Long Are Cats Pregnant?

The average cat pregnancy lasts approximately 65 days from conception. Most healthy cats deliver their kittens within a few days of this average period.

While every cat is different, veterinarians commonly use 65 days as the standard gestation length when estimating a due date.

Early Pregnancy Signs

During the first few weeks, pregnancy can be difficult to detect. Some cats may experience increased appetite, changes in behavior, and enlarged nipples.

  • Increased appetite
  • More affection toward owners
  • Enlarged or pink nipples
  • Reduced activity levels
  • Mild weight gain

Mid-Pregnancy Development

Around the fourth week, the kittens continue developing rapidly. The mother's abdomen may begin to enlarge, and weight gain becomes more noticeable.

Many owners first realize their cat is pregnant during this stage because physical changes become easier to observe.

Week-by-Week Cat Pregnancy Timeline

Weeks 1–2

During the first two weeks after mating, fertilized eggs travel through the reproductive tract and begin developing. Most cats show very few visible signs during this stage.

Weeks 3–4

The nipples often become larger and pinker, a change commonly called "pinking up." Appetite may increase and the cat may become more affectionate.

Weeks 5–6

The abdomen becomes noticeably larger as the kittens continue developing. Weight gain becomes more obvious and nutritional requirements increase.

Weeks 7–9

The kittens grow rapidly during the final weeks. Nesting behavior often appears, and the mother cat may seek quiet places to rest and prepare for delivery.

Late Pregnancy Stage

During the final weeks, the kittens grow quickly and the mother may spend more time resting. Nesting behavior often appears as the due date approaches.

Providing a quiet and comfortable environment becomes increasingly important during the final stages of pregnancy.

Signs Labor Is Near

  • Restlessness
  • Nesting behavior
  • Decreased appetite
  • Increased vocalization
  • Seeking quiet locations
  • Frequent grooming

Preparing for Delivery

Provide a quiet, clean, and comfortable area where the mother cat can safely give birth. Monitor her closely and contact a veterinarian if you notice signs of distress or complications.

A nesting box lined with clean blankets or towels can help provide a secure space for delivery.

When to Contact a Veterinarian

If labor lasts unusually long, the mother appears distressed, or there are concerns about the kittens, professional veterinary assistance should be sought immediately.

Veterinary advice is especially important if the cat stops eating, develops unusual discharge, or experiences prolonged contractions without delivering kittens.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long are cats pregnant?

Most cats are pregnant for approximately 63 to 67 days, with 65 days being a commonly used average.

How accurate is a cat pregnancy due date estimate?

A pregnancy calculator provides an estimate. Actual delivery dates may vary by several days.

When do cats start showing pregnancy signs?

Many cats begin showing visible signs of pregnancy within two to three weeks after mating.

How many kittens can a cat have?

A typical litter contains three to five kittens, although litter size varies depending on the individual cat.

What should I feed a pregnant cat?

Pregnant cats generally require higher-quality nutrition and increased calorie intake. Consult your veterinarian for specific recommendations.

When should I contact a veterinarian?

Contact a veterinarian if your cat shows signs of illness, distress, prolonged labor, or any unusual symptoms during pregnancy.

Can first-time cats have healthy pregnancies?

Yes. Many first-time mothers have normal pregnancies and healthy litters when properly cared for.
