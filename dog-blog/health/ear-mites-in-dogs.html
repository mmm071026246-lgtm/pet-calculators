Ear Mites in Dogs

Ear mites are tiny parasites that can cause a lot of discomfort in a dog’s ears. They are especially common in puppies and young dogs, but they can affect dogs of any age. These mites live in the ear canal and often cause intense itching, head shaking, and irritation that can become quite noticeable very quickly.

Important: Ear mites can cause significant discomfort and may need timely treatment rather than simple home observation.

Common signs of ear mites

Dogs with ear mites may show signs such as:

  • Frequent scratching at the ears
  • Head shaking or head tilting
  • Dark, crumbly debris in the ear
  • Redness or swelling inside the ear
  • Bad smell or unusual discharge
  • Restlessness or sensitivity around the ears

Some dogs become very uncomfortable and may seem more irritable than usual because the itching can be constant. In severe cases, the ear canal may become inflamed and more sensitive to touch.

How ear mites are spread

Ear mites are contagious and can spread between pets, especially when dogs live together or spend time in close contact. They can also be shared through bedding, grooming tools, or shared environments. If one pet has ear mites, other pets in the household may need to be checked as well.

This is why treating only one dog may not be enough if there are other pets at home.

Why ear mites should not be ignored

Ear mites can cause ongoing itching and irritation, and repeated scratching may lead to self-inflicted injuries around the ear. The ear canal can also become inflamed, making the dog more uncomfortable and increasing the chance of a secondary infection.

If the problem is not addressed, it can become harder to manage and may affect the dog’s comfort and sleep. Early treatment is usually the smoother option.

How a veterinarian may diagnose them

A veterinarian may look inside the ear, examine the debris, and determine whether mites are present. In some cases, they may recommend a treatment plan that includes medication, cleaning, and follow-up care. Because ear mites can look similar to other ear problems, a proper exam is important.

The diagnosis process may also help rule out infections or allergies that cause similar symptoms. That matters because the treatment approach can be different depending on the cause.

Typical treatment options

Treatment usually focuses on eliminating the mites and reducing the inflammation in the ear. A veterinarian may recommend a parasite treatment, ear cleaning, or prescription medication depending on how severe the problem is. It is important to follow the treatment plan carefully, because stopping early can lead to a return of symptoms.

Because mites may be present in the environment or on other pets, follow-up care and checking household pets can be part of the plan.

Home care and prevention

Home care should be gentle and should not replace veterinary advice. It may help to keep the ears clean and dry, avoid excessive scratching, and prevent the dog from rubbing the ears too aggressively. A calm environment and soft bedding can also help the dog rest while it is healing.

To reduce the chance of reinfestation, it can help to wash bedding, vacuum nearby areas, and check other pets in the household. Prevention is easier when the whole environment is addressed, not just the affected dog.

How owners often notice the problem

Many owners first notice ear mites because a dog suddenly becomes much more interested in scratching one or both ears. The behavior may seem minor at first, but it can escalate quickly into persistent head shaking and obvious irritation. Dogs that are already prone to ear sensitivity may react more strongly than others.

Some dogs also begin rubbing their ears on furniture or carpets because the itching feels constant. That behavior can make the problem seem more like a general irritation than an ear issue, which is why careful observation matters.

What makes a dog more likely to get ear mites

Young dogs, puppies, and dogs that spend time around other pets may be more likely to get ear mites. Stress, poor grooming, or a crowded household can also make it easier for the parasites to spread. Dogs with skin problems or ongoing ear irritation may be more prone to repeated issues as well.

Because mites often spread through close contact, a dog that lives with other pets should be checked even if only one dog is showing obvious symptoms. Treating only one pet can leave the problem behind in the household.

What good follow-up care looks like

After treatment begins, owners should keep an eye on the dog’s comfort level and ear appearance. If the scratching and head shaking improve, that is a good sign, but improvement should still be monitored because some cases need more than one round of care. A follow-up veterinary visit can help confirm that the mites are truly gone and that no secondary infection has developed.

It also helps to keep the ear area clean and dry during recovery, as repeated irritation can slow healing. Avoid vigorous scratching or rubbing, and keep the dog’s environment calm while the ear settles.

Why ear mites can come back

Ear mites can return if the treatment plan is not completed, if other pets are still carrying them, or if the environment is not addressed. Some dogs improve at first and then seem to relapse because the source of the problem was never fully handled. That is why follow-up care and checking other pets in the household can be just as important as the initial treatment.

In some cases, repeated scratching or irritation can leave the ear more sensitive even after the mites are gone. That means owners may need to watch for lingering discomfort and continue to support the ear gently while it heals.

What owners can do between vet visits

Between visits, keep the ear area clean and dry, avoid repeated rubbing, and prevent the dog from scratching aggressively. A calm environment can reduce stress and help the dog rest more comfortably while treatment takes effect. If the dog seems to be getting worse rather than better, it is better to contact a veterinarian sooner rather than later.

It can also help to keep a short note of when symptoms started, whether the dog has been around other animals, and whether the behavior seems to be improving or worsening day by day. These details can make future veterinary visits more useful.

What to watch for after treatment starts

After treatment begins, the most important thing is to monitor whether the dog’s behavior improves over the next few days. If the scratching becomes less frequent, the head shaking slows down, and the dog seems calmer around the ears, that usually indicates the treatment is helping. On the other hand, if the dog seems more uncomfortable, the ear becomes more red, or a strong odor develops, it is worth contacting a veterinarian to make sure the problem has not progressed.

Some dogs need more than one round of treatment or additional follow-up care, especially if the ear was already irritated before the mites were identified. That is why paying attention to changes over time is more helpful than judging the situation based on a single day.

How to reduce the chance of reinfestation

Reinfestation is more likely when the entire household is not considered. If one pet is treated but another pet in the home still has mites, the problem may return. For that reason, checking all household pets and cleaning shared bedding and grooming items can be a smart part of the plan.

Keeping the dog’s ears dry, avoiding repeated irritation, and making sure the dog is comfortable while recovering can also help prevent a cycle of scratching and worsening symptoms.

When the problem may be more than just mites

Some ear problems look similar to ear mites, but the cause may be a different issue such as a bacterial or yeast infection, an allergy, or skin irritation. That is one reason a veterinarian may want to examine the ear directly rather than assume the symptoms are caused only by mites. If the dog has a lot of discharge, persistent odor, or very intense inflammation, the treatment plan may need to be adjusted.

Because mites can make an ear more irritated, dogs may develop secondary issues that need extra care. This is especially common when the dog has been scratching repeatedly or the ear canal is already inflamed before treatment begins.

How to reduce discomfort while the ear heals

While treatment is underway, keeping the dog comfortable matters just as much as eliminating the mites. A quiet space, soft bedding, and limited rough play can all help the dog rest and reduce extra irritation. Owners should also avoid rubbing or scratching the ear area, because that can worsen the discomfort and slow recovery.

It can be helpful to observe whether the dog seems calmer at night, shakes its head less often, or stops rubbing its ears against furniture. Those changes are encouraging signs that the treatment is making a difference.

Frequently asked questions

Can ear mites be seen with the naked eye?

They are tiny, so they are often not visible without magnification, but the debris and irritation they cause are usually noticeable.

Can ear mites spread to humans?

Ear mites are primarily a pet problem and are not usually a major concern for people, but it is still best to avoid handling an infected ear area without washing hands.

Do ear mites always cause a bad smell?

No, but they can cause irritation, itching, and discharge that sometimes lead to odor.

Bottom line

Ear mites in dogs are uncomfortable and can spread, but they are treatable when addressed early. Careful observation, proper treatment, and attention to other pets in the household can help your dog recover more quickly and avoid repeat problems.
